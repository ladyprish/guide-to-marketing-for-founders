No. 1: Strategic Foundation
1

Make product marketing your first problem to solve.

Over the past decade or so, I've found hiring a product marketer first leads to more consistent success.

— Tomasz Tunguz, Venture Capitalist

At its core, product marketing is understanding your customer, clarifying the message, and mapping it to the moments that matter. It's the strategic work that should happen before execution (and usually doesn't).

Here's the thing: When you skip the foundational work, you waste budget on a lot of activity that creates noise, but doesn't convert.

Prioritize it and everything else — outbound, content, partnerships, paid ads — gets more effective because it's been pressure-tested in real conversations.

Think: positioning first, execution second — or you'll spend twice.
The Core of Great Product Marketing
Four pillars to get right
ICP & Segmentation Who you're for (and not) and which wedge you'll win first
Positioning & Messaging How you frame the market and differentiate your product
Product Offering Which use cases you lead with and how you package value
Sales Enablement What you arm sales with to make the story repeatable
Get these four right before you hire your first demand gen person. They give every channel a fighting chance to convert.
No. 2: GTM Experiments
2

Treat your GTM motion like a loop — not a launch.

In 0→1, you can't spend months planning a "perfect" launch. You need signal — and fast. That's why I'm a big believer in GTM sprints: time-boxed cycles where you place small bets, learn quickly, and turn what works into a repeatable play.

Here's the thing: The market won't wait for your plan. If you're not learning quickly, you're just guessing...very, very slowly.

Think: small bets, fast learning loops, and repeatable GTM plays.
A Strong GTM Sprint
Process Includes →
Five steps to run each cycle
1Align on a goal for the sprint.
2Pick one clear hypothesis.
3Run focused experiments.
4Capture signal & track what moves.
5Roll findings into the next sprint.
I've run GTM sprints as short as two weeks and as long as six, depending on the hypothesis. The point isn't the timeline — it's the tempo: hypothesize → test → learn → tighten → repeat.
No. 3: Funnel Repair
3

Fix your leaky bucket before you scale demand.

When opportunities feel like they're disappearing or your churn rate is high, the instinct is to pour on more demand—more outbound, more content, more spend.

But in 0→1, that's often the most expensive way to learn.

Here's the thing: More leads won't save a broken message, a fuzzy ICP, or a demo that doesn't convert.

If prospects are stalling, ghosting, or churning, the problem usually isn't volume; it's a leaky bucket, and you need to focus on repairing your funnel before you scale demand. Otherwise, you'll push good leads into a broken process.

When you patch those leaks, every lead becomes more valuable, your sales cycle shortens, and growth starts to compound.

Think: conversion rate, not lead volume — optimize your funnel from the bottom up.
How To Repair Your Funnel
Four places to look first
Fix Your Demo No feature tour. Show one workflow and one outcome.
Build Your Champion Equip an internal buyer to sell, even if you're not in the room.
Control Your Sales Process Get the economic buyer into the room early, not at the end.
Protect Retention Tighten onboarding and feed friction back to product & CS.
Start here before adding budget. Each fix compounds — a better demo closes faster, a stronger champion reduces ghosting, and tighter onboarding protects the revenue you already have.
